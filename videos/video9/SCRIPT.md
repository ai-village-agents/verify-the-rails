# Script - Video 9

## Title

The Winners Are Not the Whole Field: How Survivorship Bias Misleads

## Promise

Visible winners are real evidence.
They show something is possible.
They do not show how common that outcome is.

This episode explains survivorship bias in plain terms and gives a practical way to check winner-heavy claims before repeating them.

## Cold Open

A founder shares a success thread:
`I built three products, learned fast, then hit a breakout.`

A tools list says:
`These are the products every serious team uses now.`

A creator post says:
`I followed these habits and grew. Do this and you can too.`

All three can be true.
All three can still mislead if they hide everyone who disappeared along the way.

## Reframe

On Verify the Rails, we keep one boundary clear:

- survivors prove possibility
- prevalence needs the full field

If only survivors are visible, we are looking at filtered outcomes.
That can support a `can happen` claim.
It cannot, by itself, support a `usually happens` claim.

## Core Framework

Use three rails:

- `Observed`: what is directly visible now
- `Inference`: what conclusion is being drawn
- `Unknown`: what was filtered out before observation

Survivorship bias appears when the unknown attrition is treated as irrelevant.

## Worked Example 1: Founder Success Thread

A founder posts a polished timeline after a major outcome.
The thread is detailed, honest, and useful.
It describes decisions, pivots, and one winning launch.

`Observed`: one founder achieved a strong result.
`Observed`: the thread documents real tactics used in that path.
`Inference`: those tactics are broadly reliable for most founders.
`Unknown`: how many similar attempts used similar tactics and failed anyway.

The thread can be accurate while still non-representative.
It proves one path worked, not how often it works.

## Worked Example 2: Current Tools List

A market map lists current category leaders.
It highlights products still active, funded, and visible.
The page calls the list a guide to `what wins`.

`Observed`: these products are currently surviving and used by real teams.
`Inference`: their shared traits are the main reason products succeed in this category.
`Unknown`: discontinued, sunset, or abandoned peers that had similar traits but did not survive.

A survivors-only map hides the graveyard.
Without the graveyard, causal lessons are unstable.

## Worked Example 3: Creator Advice From Visible Accounts

A creator analyzes high-growth channels and extracts common habits.
The advice sounds practical: post cadence, thumbnail style, topic framing.
All source accounts are still active and easy to inspect.

`Observed`: visible accounts with growth often share those habits.
`Inference`: those habits are likely enough for most creators to grow.
`Unknown`: the many creators who followed similar habits, saw weak results, and became inactive or invisible.

When silent failures are absent, confidence inflates.
The advice may still help, but expected outcomes are often overstated.

## Verification Routine

Before accepting a winner-heavy lesson, run this short check:

1. Name the starting population, not just visible survivors.
2. Ask what attrition happened before the sample reached you.
3. Find at least one source about discontinued, failed, or inactive cases.
4. Separate `possible` claims from `typical` claims.
5. Write one O-I-U note in plain language.

## Reusable O-I-U Note

`Observed`: surviving examples are real and informative.
`Inference`: these examples describe what usually happens.
`Unknown`: denominator, attrition path, and outcomes of non-survivors.

## Close

Survivors matter.
They are not noise, and they are not fake.

But they are a filtered slice of the field.
If the field is hidden, prevalence claims are weak.

Visible survivors are real evidence.
They are not the whole distribution.
