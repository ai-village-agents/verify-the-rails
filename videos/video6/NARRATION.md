# Narration - Video 6

A post says, "24,000 reports filed this month."
The number is real.
The screenshot is real.
The conclusion can still be wrong.

A real number can mislead when context is missing.
The usual gaps are denominator, baseline, and time window.

On Verify the Rails, we start with evidence before interpretation.
So we do not stop at "Is this number real?"
We ask what it can actually prove.

Most weak number claims are not fake numbers.
They are incomplete numbers.

A count alone does not tell you prevalence.
A percent change alone does not tell you scale.
A city total alone does not tell you per-capita burden.
A cumulative total alone does not tell you current pace.

That is why we separate observed fact from inference.
Observed fact is what the source directly states.
Inference is the conclusion someone draws from it.
If context is missing, confidence should shrink.

First example.
A claim says, "24,000 safety reports in one month."
That sounds large.
Now ask: out of how many?

Add one more line: there were 18 million active users that month.
Now the same count is about 0.13 percent, or 1.3 per 1,000 users.

Observed fact: 24,000 reports.
Observed fact: 18 million users.
Inference: "the platform is collapsing" does not follow from count alone.
Maybe the rate is high.
Maybe it is low.
You still need a baseline to know whether this month is unusual.

Second example.
A claim says, "Incidents doubled, up 100 percent."
The math can be correct.
But doubled from what?

If the count moved from 3 cases to 6 cases, that is exactly 100 percent growth.
No arithmetic issue.
But the base is tiny.

Observed fact: 3 to 6.
Observed fact: that is doubling.
Inference: "the problem is now widespread" is not justified by those two values alone.
To support that claim, you need denominator and more trend points.

Third example.
A claim says, "City A has more complaints than City B, so City A is worse."
Suppose City A has 12,000 complaints and City B has 8,000.
If we stop there, City A looks worse.
Now add population.
City A has 4 million residents.
City B has 1 million.

Per 1,000 residents, City A is about 3.
City B is about 8.
The ranking flips when we use per-capita rates.

Observed fact: City A has the larger raw count.
Observed fact: City B has the higher rate.
Inference: "City A has the bigger problem" depends on the metric tied to the claim.
If the claim is prevalence, rate is usually the right comparison.

Fourth example.
A claim says, "1,200 incidents found."
Over what window?

If that total covers five years, it averages about 240 per year, around 20 per month.
The same number feels different once period is visible.

Observed fact: 1,200 incidents.
Observed fact: five-year window.
Inference: "this month is spiraling" is unsupported unless month-level data shows a current spike.
A long-window total cannot prove short-window acceleration by itself.

When a number looks decisive, run four questions.
Out of how many?
Compared with what baseline?
Over what time window?
Is this a count or a rate?

Then mark claim limits.
Observed: what the source says.
Inference: what it may imply.
Unknown: what is still missing.

That is uncertainty discipline.
It keeps confidence aligned with evidence.

Verify the Rails is not about bigger numbers.
It is about better verification.

Before you share, check denominator, baseline, and time window.
Then check whether the claim needs rate, not just count.

Because a real number can still carry an incomplete conclusion.
Real number, incomplete conclusion.
