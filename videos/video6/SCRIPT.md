# Script - Video 6

## Title

A Real Number, Incomplete Conclusion

## Cold Open

A post says, "24,000 reports filed this month."
The number is real.
The screenshot is real.
The conclusion can still be wrong.

That sounds strange, but it happens constantly.
A real number can mislead when key context is missing.
The usual gaps are denominator, baseline, and time window.

On Verify the Rails, we use one rule first: evidence before interpretation.
So we do not stop at "Is this number real?"
We ask what the number can actually prove.

## Reframe

Most weak number claims are not fake numbers.
They are incomplete numbers.

A count by itself does not tell you prevalence.
A percentage change by itself does not tell you scale.
A city total by itself does not tell you per-capita burden.
A cumulative total by itself does not tell you current pace.

So the right move is to separate observed fact from inference.
Observed fact is what the source directly states.
Inference is the conclusion someone draws from it.
If context is missing, confidence should shrink.

## Worked Example 1: Large Count Without Denominator

Start with this claim: "24,000 safety reports in one month."
That sounds large.
But out of how many users?

Add context: the service had 18 million active users that month.
Now the same count becomes about 0.13 percent, or 1.3 per 1,000 users.

Observed fact: 24,000 reports were filed.
Observed fact: there were 18 million active users.

Inference: "the platform is collapsing" does not follow from count alone.
Maybe the rate is high.
Maybe it is low.
You still need a baseline to decide.

So a big count can be real and still be incomplete evidence.
Without denominator, magnitude is hard to interpret.

## Worked Example 2: Doubled Percentage From a Tiny Base

Now a second claim: "Incidents doubled, up 100 percent."
The math may be correct.
But doubled from what starting point?

If the count moved from 3 cases to 6 cases, that is exactly a 100 percent increase.
No arithmetic error.
But the base is tiny.

Observed fact: the count rose from 3 to 6.
Observed fact: that is a doubling.

Inference: "the problem is now widespread" is not justified by two small values.
To support that claim, you need denominator, broader trend, and possibly severity context.

Percent change is useful.
It is just incomplete without base level.

## Worked Example 3: City Comparison Without Per-Capita Adjustment

Third claim: "City A has more complaints than City B, so City A is worse."
Suppose City A has 12,000 complaints and City B has 8,000.
If you stop there, City A looks worse.

Now include population.
City A has 4 million residents.
City B has 1 million residents.

Per 1,000 residents, City A is about 3.
City B is about 8.
The comparison reverses once denominator is aligned.

Observed fact: City A has a larger raw count.
Observed fact: City B has a higher per-capita rate.

Inference: "City A has the larger problem" depends on whether the claim is about total volume or prevalence.
If the claim is prevalence, rate is the better measure.

## Worked Example 4: Total Spread Over a Long Time Window

Fourth claim: "1,200 incidents found."
Again, that can be a real number.
But over what time window?

If 1,200 covers five years, that averages about 240 per year, around 20 per month.
The same total looks different once period is visible.

Observed fact: 1,200 incidents were recorded.
Observed fact: the window is five years.

Inference: "this month is spiraling" is unsupported unless month-level data shows current acceleration.
A long-window total cannot prove short-window surge by itself.

Time window is not a side detail.
It controls what trend claim is defensible.

## Reusable Checklist

When a number looks decisive, run four questions:

1. Out of how many?
2. Compared with what baseline?
3. Over what time window?
4. Is this a count or a rate?

Then mark claim boundaries in plain language.

Observed: what the source directly says.
Inference: what you think it implies.
Unknown: what is still missing.

That is uncertainty discipline.
It does not make you indecisive.
It makes your confidence proportional to evidence.

## Closing

Verify the Rails is not about winning with bigger numbers.
It is about making conclusions that match what is actually observed.

Before you repost a dramatic statistic, check denominator, baseline, and window.
Then check whether the claim needs a rate instead of a count.

Because a real number can still carry an incomplete conclusion.
Real number, incomplete conclusion.
