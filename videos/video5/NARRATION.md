# Narration - Video 5

Two screenshots can contain real information and still support the wrong conclusion.

Sometimes the problem is not fake pixels. Sometimes the problem is the crop.

A screenshot only shows what was inside the frame. And if the frame cuts off the date, the filter, the domain, the draft label, or the update banner, the missing context can matter more than the visible text.

That means a screenshot can be authentic while the claim attached to it still goes too far.

A useful first question is not just, is this image real? A more useful question is, what might be outside the crop?

Start with a chart. Suppose a screenshot shows a dashboard tile that reads twenty-four thousand users. People repost it as proof that the product has exactly twenty-four thousand total users. But then the uncropped version shows a filter in the corner: last twenty-four hours. The screenshot was real. The number was real. But the claim changed because the crop hid what the number counted.

Now look at a status page. A cropped image shows investigating elevated error rates. Shared alone, it suggests the incident is ongoing. But the full page includes a banner above the crop that says resolved twenty minutes later. Again, the earlier crop was real. What changed is the current-state claim someone tried to make from it.

Now consider a document screenshot. The quoted paragraph is word-for-word accurate. But the uncropped header says draft for review. If someone posts that paragraph as if it came from the final adopted policy, the problem is not fabricated text. The problem is that the crop removed the status label that tells you what stage of the document you are looking at.

And then there is domain context. A screenshot may look like an official notice, but the address bar or footer outside the crop reveals that the page is a mirror, a repost, or a commentary site rather than the primary source. The visible text may still be copied correctly. But the source claim changes once you know where it came from.

These are different cases, but the pattern is the same. A crop can hide the clue that sets the scope, time, status, or provenance of the evidence.

So here is a fast routine.

Step one: write down the exact claim the screenshot is being used to support.

Step two: look for clues that might be missing outside the frame. Was the date cropped out? The domain? The filter? A warning banner? A document header? A footnote?

Step three: find the uncropped source if possible. Open the page, ask for the full screenshot, or compare against a current version.

Step four: separate what the screenshot directly shows from what depends on missing context.

Step five: if the missing context is unresolved, lower your confidence before sharing.

A good note sounds like this. Observed: the cropped screenshot shows twenty-four thousand users. Unknown: whether that means total users, active users, or a filtered window. Inference: the repost may be overclaiming unless the full view confirms the metric definition.

That wording is slower, but it is also more honest.

A crop is not proof of deception. Sometimes people crop for convenience. Sometimes platforms crop automatically. But once context disappears, your certainty should shrink with it.

So before you repost a screenshot, ask one more question.

What is outside the frame?

Because online, the missing clue is often the difference between a real image and a wrong conclusion.
