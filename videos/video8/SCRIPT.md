# Script - Video 8

## Title

Three Posts Are Not a Trend: How Sample Bias Misleads

## Promise

A few authentic examples can prove existence.
They do not automatically prove prevalence.

This episode shows how sample bias and cherry-picking turn real examples into weak population claims.

## Cold Open

You open your feed and see three unusual posts in a row.
Each has huge engagement.
A creator says: `This is everywhere now.`

Then a thread appears with five screenshots and the caption:
`Everyone is saying this.`

The examples may be real.
But a real example is not the same as a representative sample.

## Reframe

On Verify the Rails, we separate two questions:

1. Did this happen?
2. How common is it?

Question one is evidence of existence.
Question two is evidence of prevalence.

Most online trend errors happen when people answer question one and act like they answered question two.

## Core Framework

Keep three rails visible:

- `Observed`: what is directly shown
- `Inference`: what conclusion is being made
- `Unknown`: what is missing for a population claim

The more a claim talks about `everyone`, `most`, or `normal`, the more denominator and sampling method matter.

## Example 1: Feed Full of Unusual Viral Posts

Suppose your home feed shows multiple extreme posts on one topic.
Each post is real.
But feed ranking is not random sampling.
It is optimized for attention, recency, and engagement.

`Observed`: you can document several unusual viral posts.
`Observed`: those posts were highly amplified by ranking.
`Inference`: `this is now typical behavior online` is not established.
`Unknown`: what share of the full population those posts represent.

Your feed is a visibility surface, not a census.

## Example 2: Screenshot Thread Claiming "Everyone Is Saying This"

Now a thread presents five screenshots with the line `everyone is saying this.`
The screenshots may be authentic and correctly captured.

But selection is the key question.
How were those five chosen?
Out of how many total posts?
Were contradictory examples excluded?

`Observed`: five real posts show the claim exists.
`Inference`: the claim is dominant or mainstream.
`Unknown`: denominator, collection method, and base rate in the broader conversation.

Without sampling details, this is evidence of existence, not prevalence.

## Example 3: Reports or Moderation Dashboard

Third case: a dashboard shows a spike in reported incidents or flagged content.
Some people conclude: `the behavior itself exploded.`

But report volume is shaped by visibility and reporting intensity.
A campaign, media attention, or UI prompt can increase reports without the same proportional increase in underlying behavior.

`Observed`: reported/flagged counts increased.
`Observed`: reporting intensity or moderation focus also changed.
`Inference`: prevalence in the whole population rose by the same amount.
`Unknown`: true rate in the underlying population under stable reporting conditions.

Reports measure what gets noticed and submitted, not everything that occurred.

## Practical Verification Checklist

Before sharing a trend claim from examples, run this quick check:

1. Define the population in one sentence.
2. Count the sample and state the denominator.
3. Ask how examples were selected (random, ranked, curated, reported).
4. Look for visibility amplifiers (virality, algorithm boosts, campaigns, policy changes).
5. Search for counterexamples and silent majority context.
6. Write a short `Observed / Inference / Unknown` note.

## Reusable O-I-U Note

`Observed`: These examples are real and show the phenomenon exists.
`Inference`: The phenomenon is widespread or typical.
`Unknown`: Sampling method, denominator, and representativeness needed to support that prevalence claim.

## Close

Do not confuse `I can show examples` with `I measured the population.`

Examples are useful.
Claims about prevalence need stronger sampling evidence.

**Real examples, weak population claim.**
