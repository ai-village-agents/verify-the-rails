# Narration - Video 8

You open your feed and see three unusual posts in a row.
Each one is dramatic. Each one has huge engagement.
A caption says, "This is everywhere now."

Then you see a thread with five screenshots and a confident line: "Everyone is saying this."

This is one of the most common digital-literacy mistakes.
People prove that something exists, then talk as if they proved how common it is.

On Verify the Rails, we separate those claims.
Evidence of existence answers: did this happen at least once?
Evidence of prevalence answers: how frequent or typical is this in the larger population?

Those are different standards.
A few real examples can pass the first standard and still fail the second.

Keep three rails visible as we go: Observed, Inference, Unknown.
Observed is what the source directly shows.
Inference is the conclusion someone draws.
Unknown is what must be true before a population claim is justified.

Example one: a feed full of unusual viral posts.
Suppose you open your app and see multiple extreme posts on the same topic.
All of them are real posts from real accounts.
No fabrication is needed.

The problem is sampling.
Your feed is not a random draw from all posts.
It is a ranked surface, usually tuned for attention signals like engagement, novelty, recency, and network effects.
That means unusual content can be overrepresented in what you see.

Observed: several unusual posts appeared and went viral.
Observed: the platform amplified them.
Inference: "this is typical behavior across the whole user base" is not established.
Unknown: denominator and share of total posts that match this pattern.

Visible is not the same as common.

Example two: a screenshot thread claiming "everyone is saying this."
The thread shows five extreme screenshots.
Again, each screenshot may be authentic.
The events happened.

But authenticity does not solve representativeness.
How were these five selected?
Were they chosen from fifty posts, five thousand, or fifty thousand?
Were counterexamples excluded?
Was the search window narrow and peak-biased?

Observed: five real posts demonstrate existence of the claim.
Inference: the claim is dominant, mainstream, or universal.
Unknown: sampling method, denominator, and the rate of opposing or neutral posts in the same population.

Without that missing context, "everyone" is rhetorical, not measured.

Example three: a reports or moderation dashboard.
A chart shows reported incidents or flagged content rising sharply.
A post says, "Proof this behavior is exploding."

Sometimes it is rising.
But dashboard visibility is shaped by reporting intensity.
If media attention spikes, if a call-to-report campaign runs, if a report button becomes easier to find, or if moderators prioritize one category, reported volume can jump even when underlying prevalence changes less.

Observed: reports or flags increased.
Observed: reporting pressure, interface prompts, or moderation focus also changed.
Inference: true prevalence in the full population increased by the same magnitude.
Unknown: stable-rate measurement across comparable reporting conditions.

So the dashboard can be accurate about what was reported, while still weak evidence about how common the behavior is overall.

Across all three examples, the pattern is consistent.
Feed examples, screenshot collages, and report dashboards can all provide real signals.
But they are often selected, amplified, or filtered by visibility systems.
When that happens, they are evidence that something happened, not enough evidence for how widespread it is.

Use this quick verification checklist before you share a trend claim.

One: define the population clearly.
Are we talking about all users, one platform, one region, one week, or one community?

Two: state sample size and denominator.
If you cannot say "X out of Y," your prevalence claim is probably under-evidenced.

Three: identify collection method.
Was this random sampling, curated examples, ranked feed output, or user reports?

Four: map visibility pressures.
Check virality loops, recommendation boosts, campaigns, policy changes, and interface nudges that affect what gets seen or submitted.

Five: look for missing context.
Search for ordinary cases, counterexamples, and baseline rates.

Six: write a short Observed, Inference, Unknown note before making a broad claim.

Observed: these examples are real and show existence.
Inference: this pattern is prevalent.
Unknown: whether the sample is representative enough to support that population claim.

This approach is not about dismissing examples.
Examples matter.
They help detect new issues and surface real harm.
But if the claim is about scale, typicality, or "everyone," the evidence bar is higher.

Ask one final question before you share:
Did I collect representative evidence, or did I collect memorable evidence?

That single question prevents a lot of overreach.

Real examples, weak population claim.
