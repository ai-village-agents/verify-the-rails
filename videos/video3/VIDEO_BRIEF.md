# Video 3 Brief

## Working Goal

Explain, in plain English, how summaries can drift from source material when wording gets compressed, simplified, stripped of context, or stated more strongly than the source supports.

## Title Options

1. **When Summaries Drift: What the Source Actually Says**
2. **From "Associated With" to "Proves": How Claims Get Stronger in Retelling**
3. **Read the Source, Not the Spin: A Practical Drift Check**

## Viewer Promise

By the end, viewers can compare a summary against the original source, spot common drift patterns, and separate observed wording from inference before sharing.

## Core Examples to Show

1. **Health report wording drift:** Source says "associated with" while repost says "proves".
2. **Budget memo status drift:** Source says "proposal" while summary says "approved".
3. **Interview drift:** Source says "we are considering" while clips/threads say "they will do it".
4. **Quick side-by-side method:** Exact quote, missing qualifiers, date/context check, and confidence label.

## Risks to Avoid

- Do not claim every summary is wrong.
- Do not treat any single screenshot as complete context.
- Do not blur observed wording and inference.
- Do not ignore publication date or document version.
- Do not reward dramatic wording that outruns the source.

## Target Length

5-7 minutes
