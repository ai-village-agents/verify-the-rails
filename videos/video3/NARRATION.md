# When Summaries Drift From Sources

## 0:00-0:40 - Cold Open: Same Source, Stronger Summary

A report headline says: "Daily soda intake was associated with higher sleep complaints in this sample."

A repost says: "New report proves soda causes poor sleep."

Those are not the same claim.

Observed fact: the source uses "associated with."

Observed fact: the repost uses "proves" and "causes."

Inference: the repost is stronger than the source text directly supports.

This does not mean every summary is wrong. It means wording strength can drift as people compress and retell.

## 0:40-1:20 - Reframe: Compare Claims, Not Tone

The goal here is not to dunk on summaries.

Good summaries are useful. Most people do not have time to read full reports every day.

The problem is not summarizing. The problem is when the summary outruns what the source actually says.

Observed fact: people usually share the shorter version first.

Inference: speed and brevity make drift more likely, especially when a post rewards certainty.

So the question is simple: does the summary preserve the source claim, or strengthen it?

We can test that without being cynical and without pretending every mismatch is deception.

## 1:20-2:05 - Source Hierarchy: Start at the Strongest Evidence

Use a basic hierarchy.

Primary source first: the report, interview transcript, budget memo, or official release.

Secondary summary next: article, thread, newsletter, or clip caption.

Commentary after that: posts reacting to the summary.

Observed fact: each retelling layer has fewer details than the layer before it.

Inference: the farther you are from the primary text, the higher the risk that qualifiers are missing.

When you can, compare exact wording between layer one and layer two before deciding what is true.

## 2:05-3:15 - Five Common Drift Mechanisms

Here are five common ways summaries drift.

One: compression. A detailed sentence becomes a short slogan.

Two: omission. Qualifiers like "in this sample" or "may" get dropped.

Three: simplification. Technical wording is translated into plain language, sometimes too aggressively.

Four: outdated context. A summary quotes an older draft while a newer version changed key wording.

Five: wording that outruns the source. "Proposal" becomes "approved." "Considering" becomes "will."

Observed fact: these shifts are visible in exact word choices.

Inference: once these words change, perceived certainty usually increases even if evidence did not.

## 3:15-4:45 - Worked Comparison: Health Report + Budget Memo

Let’s run a side-by-side check.

Example one, health report.

Source text: "Higher screen time was associated with more frequent headaches in surveyed adults."

Summary text: "Study proves screen time causes headaches."

Observed fact: source says "associated with."

Observed fact: source scope is "surveyed adults," not all people.

Observed fact: summary swaps in "proves" and "causes," and drops scope.

Inference: the summary claim is overstated relative to the source.

Example two, budget memo.

Source text: "The department proposes a pilot transit subsidy beginning next fiscal year."

Summary text: "City approved free transit next year."

Observed fact: source says "proposes," which indicates a plan under consideration.

Observed fact: summary says "approved," which indicates a decision already made.

Inference: status drift changed the claim from possible future action to confirmed policy.

Same method, two domains: compare verbs, scope words, and status words.

## 4:45-5:55 - Practical Verification Routine

Here is a fast routine you can use before sharing.

Step one. Capture the exact source sentence and the exact summary sentence.

Step two. Mark date and version for both.

Step three. Circle claim verbs: words like "associated," "suggests," "proposes," "approved," "proves," "causes," "will."

Step four. Check scope words: sample, location, timeframe, and population.

Step five. Write two lines.

Observed: quote what each text literally says.

Inference: state what you think it means, with a confidence label like likely, possible, or unknown.

That format keeps your reasoning transparent and makes corrections easier if new context appears.

## 5:55-6:35 - Reusable Verification Checklist and Close

Use this checklist any time a summary feels stronger than the source.

1. Find the primary source, not just reposts.
2. Compare exact wording side by side.
3. Check verbs for strength shifts.
4. Check scope words and missing qualifiers.
5. Check date and version context.
6. Separate observed text from inference.
7. Label confidence before sharing.

This process will not catch everything.

But it reliably reduces overconfident claims and helps you stay fair, specific, and evidence-first when claims conflict online.
