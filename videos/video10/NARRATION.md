# Narration - Video 10

One Chart Is Not the Whole Story: How Axis and Framing Choices Mislead.

A chart can use accurate data and still leave you with the wrong takeaway.
That is not always fraud.
Often it is framing.
Small design choices can push perception in a direction the data alone does not require.

This episode is about a simple claim:
real numbers can still produce a distorted visual impression.

To stay precise, we will use one reusable framework:
Observed, Impression, Unknown.

Observed is what the chart directly shows.
Impression is what the framing makes you feel is happening.
Unknown is what context is missing before a stronger claim is justified.

Example one: truncated y-axis.

Imagine a bar chart comparing two months of defect rate in a process.
Month A is 4.8 percent and Month B is 5.2 percent.
That is a 0.4 percentage-point difference.
Real, but small.

Now imagine the y-axis starts at 4.6 instead of 0.
Suddenly one bar looks several times taller than the other.
Your eye sees a dramatic jump.
But the underlying change is still 0.4 points.

Observed: the values are 4.8 and 5.2, and Month B is higher.
Impression: performance collapsed or risk exploded.
Unknown: whether the absolute magnitude is large enough to support alarm, and how that change compares with normal variation.

A truncated axis is not automatically wrong.
Sometimes it helps show subtle differences.
But when the visual drama outruns the actual magnitude, viewers need explicit context.

Example two: same series, different window or scale.

Take one weekly series: 100, 101, 99, 102, 100, 101, 100, 102.
Roughly stable, with routine noise.

Version A shows only the last three points and uses a tight vertical axis from 99 to 102.
The final uptick looks sharp.
The frame feels urgent.

Version B shows the full year and uses a broader axis.
The same last three points now sit inside a mostly flat pattern.
The frame feels steady.

Neither chart changed the underlying numbers.
Only framing changed: time window and vertical scale.

Observed: all charts use the same source values.
Impression: one view implies a sudden trend, another implies background fluctuation.
Unknown: which window and scale best match the decision context, and whether the highlighted segment is representative.

This is why "what period are we looking at?" is not a cosmetic question.
It is part of claim quality.

Example three: cumulative total mistaken for acceleration.

Suppose a chart tracks cumulative support tickets over a quarter.
Every day adds around 40 new tickets.
The daily rate is roughly flat.

The cumulative line rises from left to right and may appear to bend upward.
Viewers often read this shape as "the problem is accelerating."
But cumulative totals almost always rise unless the daily rate is zero.
They are designed to accumulate.

If you plot daily new tickets beside cumulative total, the picture changes.
Daily bars look mostly level.
Now the acceleration impression weakens.

Observed: cumulative total increases steadily over time.
Impression: growth is speeding up.
Unknown: the underlying per-period rate and whether that rate is actually changing.

Cumulative charts are useful for total burden.
They are weak evidence for acceleration unless rate data is shown too.

Across these examples, the pattern repeats.
The data can be true.
The impression can still be skewed.

So before sharing a chart, run a short verification checklist.

One: check y-axis start and range.
Ask whether the baseline choice exaggerates visual distance.

Two: check time window.
Ask what happened just before and after the displayed slice.

Three: compare alternative scales.
If a small scale change flips the message, your claim needs caution.

Four: identify chart type.
Is this cumulative, per-period rate, moving average, or something else?
Do not read one type as another.

Five: write one line each for Observed, Impression, Unknown.
That step alone catches many overstatements.

Here is a reusable template:

Observed: what the chart directly reports.
Impression: what the framing encourages viewers to believe.
Unknown: what additional context is required to justify the stronger claim.

This is not about rejecting charts.
Charts are essential.
They compress information and help people reason quickly.

The goal is stricter interpretation.
When framing choices are visible, your conclusions become more reliable.
You can respect the data without overreading the design.

Real data can still support a distorted visual impression.
