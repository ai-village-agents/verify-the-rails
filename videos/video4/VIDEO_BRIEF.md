# Video Brief - Video 4

## Working Title

This Was True Yesterday: How Date and Version Drift Breaks Online Claims

## Viewer Promise

A claim can be accurately quoted and still mislead you if it is from the wrong date, version, or update state. This video will show viewers how to check whether they are looking at the current statement, an earlier revision, or a screenshot that froze a previous moment.

## Core Problem

People often argue over whether a claim is true when the real problem is that they are looking at different versions of the same page, document, dashboard, or policy. One capture may be real and historically accurate, but no longer current.

## Audience

- non-experts who see old screenshots recirculating as if current
- journalists, students, and professionals who cite webpages and public dashboards
- anyone who wants a practical way to tell current state from historical state

## Candidate Examples

1. **Policy page update drift**
   - earlier capture says "Applications close May 1"
   - current page says "Applications close May 15"
   - lesson: both can be authentic if captured at different times
2. **Release notes / status page drift**
   - status page said "Investigating" at 10:15 AM
   - later update says "Resolved" at 11:40 AM
   - lesson: quoting without time context creates false contradiction
3. **Document revision drift**
   - memo draft says "recommended"
   - final version says "approved"
   - lesson: version labels matter as much as wording
4. **Dashboard metric drift**
   - screenshot shows a lower count
   - live page now shows a higher count
   - lesson: dashboards are moving surfaces, not static facts

## Key Lessons

- ask not just "is this real?" but also "when was this true?"
- current state and historical state are different claims
- screenshots preserve moments, not always present reality
- version labels, timestamps, and update logs are evidence rails

## Reusable Checklist

1. Capture the exact claim.
2. Mark the visible date, time, timezone, or version number.
3. Compare against the current source.
4. Look for revision history, release notes, or update logs.
5. State whether the claim is current, historical, or unclear before sharing.

## Tone / Constraints

- calm and non-accusatory
- explicitly avoid implying that old screenshots are fake
- distinguish authentic historical capture from misleading current framing
- stay concrete and practical
