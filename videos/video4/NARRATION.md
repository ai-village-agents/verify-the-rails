# Narration - Video 4

Two screenshots can both be real and still point you to the wrong conclusion.

In one image, a page says applications close on May first.
In another, the same page says applications close on May fifteenth.
People rush to ask which screenshot is fake, but that is often the wrong question.

A more useful question is this: when was each version true?

Online claims do not only vary by source. They also vary by time. And if you do not check time and version context, you can share something historically accurate that is no longer current.

A lot of online disagreement is really version disagreement. A screenshot freezes one moment. A live page shows another. A draft document captures one stage. A final release captures another.

So the task is not just to ask whether a claim is real. The task is to ask whether the claim is current, historical, or unclear.

When a source changes over time, three things matter immediately: the exact wording, the timestamp or version label, and whether the person sharing it is presenting it as current or historical. If any one of those is missing, the claim gets shakier.

Imagine a scholarship page. A screenshot taken Monday says, applications close May first. A live visit on Wednesday says, applications close May fifteenth. These captures can both be authentic. The page may simply have been updated.

Observed fact: one capture shows May first and the current page shows May fifteenth. Inference: the earlier screenshot is fake. That inference does not follow. What follows is that the page changed sometime between the two observations.

So if someone posts the old screenshot today with the caption, they are still closing applications May first, that is no longer just historical evidence. It is now a current-state claim, and that current-state claim may be wrong.

Now take a service status page. At 10:15 A.M., the page says, investigating elevated error rates. At 11:40 A.M., the page says, resolved. Someone who shares the earlier screenshot at noon may technically be showing a real capture. But if they frame it as the current state, they are dropping the time context that makes the evidence meaningful.

This is why status pages, incident posts, and live blogs should always be read with time attached. Without time, an authentic update becomes a misleading summary of the present.

Now consider a memo. The draft version says, the department recommends a pilot transit subsidy next fiscal year. The final version says, the department approved a pilot transit subsidy next fiscal year.

Those are not interchangeable. A recommendation is not an approval. A draft is not a final decision. If a post quotes the draft as if it were the final adopted document, the problem is not necessarily fabricated text. The problem is version drift. The evidence came from the wrong stage of the process.

Dashboards create this problem constantly. A screenshot from yesterday may show twenty-four thousand users. The live page today may show twenty-nine thousand. Again, both can be real, but they support different claims. The screenshot supports a historical claim about what the dashboard showed at capture time. The live page supports a current claim about what the dashboard shows now. Confusion starts when people use one as proof of the other.

So here is a fast routine you can use before you repost a screenshot or cite a page.

Step one: capture the exact claim being made.

Step two: look for visible time markers — dates, times, time zones, update lines, revision numbers, draft labels, release numbers, anything that tells you where this sits in time.

Step three: check the current source. Open the live page, not just the screenshot. Ask whether the wording still matches.

Step four: look for version history. Status logs, release notes, revision histories, archived copies, or document labels like draft and final can tell you whether change has happened.

Step five: classify the claim. Is it current, historical, or unclear? That one label will often prevent overclaiming.

A good way to phrase your notes is this. Observed: this screenshot shows the page saying X at the visible date or version. Observed: the current page now says Y. Inference: the source changed between those two checks. Unknown: exactly when the change happened, unless a log confirms it.

That keeps you from saying more than the evidence supports.

An old screenshot is not always fake. A new page is not always the whole story. And a disagreement between them does not automatically mean someone is lying. Sometimes it means you are looking at different moments of the same source.

So before you share a claim, ask one extra question: when was this true?

Because on the web, the difference between current and historical is often the difference between helpful context and accidental misinformation.

Read the date. Check the version. Share with time attached.
