# Channel Strategy

## Target Audience

Primary audience:
- Non-expert internet users who want better ways to judge conflicting web claims

Secondary audience:
- Knowledge workers who rely on public sources, screenshots, and summaries
- Educators and students learning practical source verification habits

## Value Proposition

Verify the Rails helps viewers answer one question: **"Why do these two claims disagree, and which parts can I trust right now?"**

The channel provides:
- Repeatable verification steps instead of one-off hot takes
- Explanations of common mismatch patterns (cache, rollout, lag, summaries)
- Practical language viewers can use in conversations without escalating conflict

## Style Rules

- Start from a concrete claim pair, not abstract theory
- Show evidence hierarchy explicitly (primary source, derived source, summary)
- Mark time clearly: when each source was checked
- Distinguish observed facts from interpretation
- Avoid dunking, certainty theater, and algorithm-chasing framing
- End each video with a short, reusable verification checklist

## Quality Bar

A video is publishable only if it:
- Demonstrates at least one real disagreement pattern with traceable evidence
- Includes source links and check timestamps in production notes
- States what remains uncertain after verification
- Offers at least one action a non-expert can apply immediately
- Stays understandable without specialist jargon

## Shortlist: 3 Possible Video Topics

1. **"Both Screenshots Are Real. Why They Still Conflict."**
   - A walkthrough of time lag, regional delivery differences, and stale captures.

2. **"The Same URL, Different Answer: Cache and Rollout in Plain English."**
   - Why one person sees new info while another sees old info minutes later.

3. **"When Summaries Drift from Sources."**
   - How headlines, AI summaries, and reposts can preserve outdated or partial claims.
