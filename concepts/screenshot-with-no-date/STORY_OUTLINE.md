# Story Outline — The Screenshot With No Date

## Target length
Approximately 3 to 4 minutes.

## Thesis
A screenshot can be authentic and still mislead if the moment it captures is no longer the moment the audience assumes.

## Emotional arc
1. Confidence
2. Friction
3. Reinterpretation
4. Relief through method

## Beat-by-beat outline
### Beat 1 — Apparent certainty
A screenshot seems to settle the story instantly.
- viewer impression: "I understand what happened"
- narration should not interrupt too early
- let the image do persuasive work first

### Beat 2 — Small crack in the story
A missing or hidden time marker begins to matter.
- reveal that the screenshot is older than assumed
- or that it predates a follow-up update
- or that timezone ambiguity changes the reading

### Beat 3 — Reframing
Clarify the difference between three claims:
- the screenshot is real
- the screenshot shows one moment
- the reposted conclusion about the present may still be wrong

### Beat 4 — Generalization through patterns
Show 3 quick pattern types:
1. outage or incident screenshots that survive past resolution
2. dashboards or metrics where the visible window is missing
3. old posts or announcements resurfacing as if new

### Beat 5 — Practical defense
Introduce a compact routine:
- when was this captured?
- where is the original source?
- what changed after this image?
- what timezone or version context matters?

### Beat 6 — Strong closing memory
Return to the same opening screenshot, now fully contextualized.

Candidate closing line:
**"A screenshot can show a real fact from the wrong moment. Check the time before you share the story."**

## What to avoid
- too many examples with too little development
- drifting into a generic lecture about misinformation
- sounding smug toward viewers who get fooled
- overloading the ending with five different slogans

## What success would feel like
A viewer finishes the video and immediately adopts one habit: before sharing a screenshot, they look for the missing time context first.
