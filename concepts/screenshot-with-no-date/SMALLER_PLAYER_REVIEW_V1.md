# Smaller-Player Review V1 — The Screenshot With No Date

Date: 2026-05-19
Scope: narrow reduced-view spot-check of the local preview package
Artifact reviewed:
- local `preview_contact_sheet_small_v1.png` built from `preview_frames/`
- viewed in Firefox as a single reduced overview image

## Purpose
This is **not** a phone-safety claim.

This check asks a narrower question:
> When the preview frames are viewed in a noticeably reduced presentation, do the main shot ideas still survive, and which frames look most at risk of depending on text that is too small?

## Method
- Used the existing local preview frames
- Built a contact sheet with each frame reduced to roughly **480×270** before compositing
- Opened the sheet in Firefox and reviewed the overall reduced view as one page
- Treated this as a **coarse smaller-player stress check**, not as a substitute for final motion review

## Headline judgment
**The main structure survives better than the smallest details do.**

That is encouraging for concept clarity, but it does **not** clear the full readability gate.
The strongest surviving signals are:
- distinct frame titles
- big contrast shapes
- the crop → reveal logic
- the late-sequence role separation

The weakest surviving signals are:
- tiny supporting timestamps
- small explanatory side text
- some compact chips and metadata clusters

## Frame-level observations

### Frame 01 — opening crop
**Mostly survives.**

The opening title and the large amber status card still read as the main idea.
This remains one of the safer frames at smaller size.

### Frame 02 — repost velocity
**Core idea survives; supporting text looks threshold-level.**

The stacked repost-card structure is still visible, but the smallest timestamp/support text looks close to failure in a reduced overview.

### Frame 03 — crop boundary
**Most fragile of the opening trio.**

The frame concept still reads as “there is missing context outside the crop,” but the right-side explanatory text appears especially small in reduced view.
This is a likely small-player risk frame.

### Frames 04–06 — widen / resolution / O-I-U
**Main logic survives.**

The widen-and-reveal structure still reads, and the O/I/U frame remains visually distinct.
However, small metadata lines are still not something I would trust at phone size.

### Frames 07–08 — claim-scope transition
**Structure survives.**

The authentic-versus-current contrast remains visible at the layout level.
These frames appear conceptually readable even when fine text is not doing all the work.

### Frame 09 — dashboard crop
**Mixed.**

The existence of a chart dip is visible, but this frame looks dependent on the viewer already understanding the setup.
The tooltip/details are too small to count as robust smaller-player evidence by themselves.

### Frame 10 — range reveal
**Better than frame 09, but still cautionary.**

The chart + reveal structure remains legible enough to signal “the wider context changes interpretation.”
The V6 simplification appears directionally correct here.
Still, this is not strong enough evidence to relax small-player caution.

### Frame 11 — old announcement, new panic
**Survives relatively well.**

The hierarchy improvement still shows up in reduced view.
This continues to look like one of the stronger late-sequence frames.

### Frame 12 — ten-second time check
**Concept survives; fine text still borderline.**

The practical routine is visually obvious, and the frame clearly reads as a checklist / habit beat.
The smallest wording should still be treated cautiously.

### Frame 13 — closing callback
**Survives well at the structural level.**

The callback frame still reads as a return-and-summary shot rather than just another example.
The overall composition and explicit contrast structure still come through.

## Main takeaways
1. The project’s **shot roles** are holding up better than its tiniest text.
2. Frame 03 remains the most obvious smaller-player risk in the opening section.
3. Frames 10–13 continue to look like the strongest overall late-sequence run.
4. This reduced-view check supports the claim that the concept is becoming more visually legible.
5. This reduced-view check does **not** justify any phone-safety claim.

## Implications for next work
Most useful next steps remain:
1. preserve the current 10→13 baseline
2. avoid regrowing text density
3. be cautious about any frame whose meaning depends on small side text
4. if another readability pass happens, target the most fragile microcopy rather than broad redesign
