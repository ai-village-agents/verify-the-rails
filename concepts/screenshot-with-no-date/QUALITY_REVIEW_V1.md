# QUALITY REVIEW V1 — The Screenshot With No Date

## Quality-gate scoring (1-5 each)

1. **Hook — 4/5**  
   Evidence: The opening contrast is strong and immediate ("real screenshot" vs "wrong conclusion"), and the crop-to-context setup creates curiosity in the first beat. It is not a 5 yet because the opening language is still close to familiar media-literacy framing.

2. **Clarity of core idea — 4/5**  
   Evidence: The throughline is consistent across script, storyboard, and closing line: a screenshot can be authentic but temporally misleading. A viewer can likely restate the point in one sentence.

3. **Visual storytelling — 4/5**  
   Evidence: Storyboard and blueprint rely on reveal mechanics (crop -> zoom out -> timeline -> reinterpretation), not static narration support. The concept is visually taught in sequence. Not a 5 because these are planned beats, not yet validated in animatic/rough-cut execution.

4. **Distinctiveness — 3/5**  
   Evidence: O/I/U framing plus claim-scope pins are useful and somewhat specific, but parts still read like a high-quality version of a common "check context" explainer format.

5. **Viewer value — 4/5**  
   Evidence: The ten-second time-check routine is concrete and transferable (timestamp, source, timezone, later updates). This is practical same-day behavior change guidance.

6. **Pacing — 3/5**  
   Evidence: Shot timing totals 232s and allocates a long middle block to three pattern cases. Current plan risks repetition and energy drop before the practical defense section.

7. **Script quality — 4/5**  
   Evidence: Language is mostly concrete and compressed, especially in the Observed/Inference/Unknown split and pattern definitions. A few lines remain generic and can be tightened further for spoken rhythm.

8. **Finish / overall impression — 3/5**  
   Evidence: Package quality is promising and directionally better than baseline intent, but production-readiness gaps remain in the blueprint (canonical UI design, legibility decisions, motion speed standards, text density limits, asset policy choices).

**Total score: 29/40**

## Greenlight decision

**Greenlit for production today: No.**

Rationale: The package is strong in concept but currently misses full readiness on specificity and execution-risk control. Score is below the 30/40 target, and at least one non-negotiable condition is not clearly passed.

## Current strengths

- Strong central paradox: "authentic image, wrong temporal claim" is clear and reusable.
- Good reveal architecture: crop-to-context and timeline sequencing teach the logic visually.
- O/I/U framework gives structure without requiring technical jargon.
- Practical habit is short, concrete, and easy to remember.
- Opening/closing callback with the same image supports retention.

## Current weaknesses / risks

- Example set is still generic enough to feel "designed" rather than undeniably real.
- Middle section may overrun attention with repeated mechanism across three cases.
- Distinctive voice is not fully locked; portions still resemble standard explainer cadence.
- Several blueprint dependencies remain unresolved, which increases production drift risk.
- Mobile readability constraints are acknowledged but not solved in assets yet.

## Required revisions before greenlight

1. Replace or upgrade at least two pattern examples with higher-fidelity, concrete, timestamp-rich visuals that feel indisputably real in-frame.
2. Tighten runtime from 232s toward ~210-220s by compressing repetitive explanatory lines and sharpening transitions between the three pattern cases.
3. Lock the canonical status-page visual system and O/I/U color values with explicit mobile-legibility checks.
4. Produce a rough animatic to validate reveal timing, readability, and pacing before final asset build.
5. Strengthen opening language so it is unmistakably this piece, not an interchangeable media-literacy intro.
6. Define and enforce text-density limits per shot in the production package.

## What to protect in revision

- Keep the core claim exactly as a time-context error, not a generic "misinformation" lecture.
- Preserve the crop-to-zoom reveal logic; do not flatten into static cards.
- Keep the O/I/U distinction visible on-screen, not voiceover-only.
- Preserve the final callback to the original image and timeline resolution.
- Keep the practical routine short (about ten seconds), concrete, and repeatable.
