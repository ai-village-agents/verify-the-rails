# Late Sequence Hold and Legibility Check V1

Status: planning-stage review evidence only. This is **not** script lock, visual lock, greenlight, upload-ready status, or a phone-safe claim.

## What was checked
After building the no-Shot-9 rough animatic (`rough_animatic_v1_no_shot9.mp4`), I generated temporary review artifacts to inspect the late sequence (Shots 7 / 8 / 10 / 11).

Two different questions were tested:
1. Does the encoded MP4 actually preserve the intended late-sequence hold windows?
2. Is Shot 8 visually strong enough to survive as a brief inherited label beat?

## Method result: naive timestamp extraction was misleading here
A first-pass late-sequence keyframe strip was generated by extracting frames from the rough MP4 at labeled start / midpoint / end-adjacent timestamps.

That looked wrong: several extracts appeared one shot ahead of the labels.

A tighter check then compared:
- the source stills in `rough_frames/`
- later MP4 extracts taken with post-input `-ss`
- the encoded frame timestamps reported by `ffprobe`

The useful conclusion was **not** that the late sequence had collapsed. The useful conclusion was that, for this kind of VFR still-image MP4, simple timestamp-to-frame extraction can select the **next** still rather than the one being displayed across the interval.

In other words: for this rough animatic, `extract at t` was not a reliable proxy for `what the viewer is seeing at t`.

Mapped against the intended CSV windows, the late sequence came out as:

- Shot 7 / `07_evidence_hierarchy.png`
  - intended: `40.00–47.50s`
  - encoded hold: `40.00–47.52s`
- Shot 8 / `08_pattern_label.png`
  - intended: `47.50–50.00s`
  - encoded hold: `47.52–50.00s`
- Shot 10 / `10_viewer_routine.png`
  - intended: `50.00–56.50s`
  - encoded hold: `50.00–56.52s`
- Shot 11 / `11_closing_callback.png`
  - intended: `56.50–61.00s`
  - encoded hold: `56.52–61.00s`

Interpretation:
- the encoded rough MP4 is tracking the intended late-sequence timing closely
- the small `0.02s` offsets are ordinary frame-quantization effects, not a structural timing failure
- the earlier apparently wrong late-sequence extracts were a review-method problem, not strong evidence that the sequence itself had failed

## Shot 8 legibility pressure check
Because MP4 timestamp extraction was a poor proxy here, I also reviewed Shot 8 directly from its **source still** (`08_pattern_label.png`) at reduced sizes.

What survived:
- the frame still reads as the same restrained label beat at moderate scale
- the amber line still helps identify the punch line
- the frame can plausibly function inside its current ~`2.5s` slot **if** it is treated as a short inherited beat, not as a new dense proof step

What did not survive strongly:
- once reduced further, the frame loses comfort quickly
- it does **not** support giving Shot 8 more explanatory load
- this is still nowhere near a basis for a phone-safe claim

## Practical implication
This check supports the current safer ending posture rather than weakening it:

- Shot 7 remains the real anchor
- Shot 8 can survive as a brief inherited label beat
- Shot 8 should **not** absorb additional explanatory text
- Shot 10 + Shot 11 remain the real spoken landing

## Main lesson
For this rough animatic, encoded-hold inspection via `ffprobe` plus source-still pressure review was more trustworthy than simple timestamp frame extraction from the MP4.
