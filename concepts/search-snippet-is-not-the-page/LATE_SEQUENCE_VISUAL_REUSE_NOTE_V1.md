# Late-Sequence Visual Reuse Note V1 — The Search Snippet Is Not the Page

## Status note
This is a planning-stage visual note.
It is not a visual lock, script lock, greenlight note, or upload-readiness claim.

## Why this exists
Shots 8, 10, and 11 remain much less visually pressure-tested than the main proof chain.
The safest move is not to design fresh interface treatments for them.
The safest move is to reuse visual language already established by Shots 1, 3, and 7.

## Core reuse principle
The late sequence should look like it belongs to the proof chain that already earned viewer trust.
That means reusing:
- the preview/page layer distinction
- the hierarchy-box language from Shot 7
- the preview-card silhouette from Shot 1
- the calm, low-density treatment already used in the rough prototype

## Shot 8 — Pattern label
### Recommended reuse source
- Shot 7 hierarchy layout

### Safest treatment
Keep the layer logic visible, then place the sentence
`The mistake was treating the preview as the page.`
on top of or directly beside that familiar layer structure.

### Why this is safer than a new treatment
The viewer has already been taught the hierarchy in Shot 7.
Shot 8 only needs to attach a name to that finished thought.
A new interface mockup would make the beat feel larger and less earned.

### Practical design implication
- preserve the stacked-layer look or a simplified two-layer echo
- do not introduce extra explanatory bullets
- do not add a new page or browser surface here

## Shot 10 — Viewer routine
### Recommended reuse source
- Shot 7 bottom habit line

### Safest treatment
Expand the existing habit line into the three-step stack already justified by the script:
1. `Open the page.`
2. `Compare the wording.`
3. `Check one update clue.`

### Why this is safer than a new treatment
The routine already exists in compressed form inside the rough hierarchy frame.
Shot 10 can therefore feel like a clarified restatement rather than a new graphic idea.

### Practical design implication
- keep the same visual tone as Shot 7
- either reveal one line at a time or land as a compact three-line stack
- avoid sub-bullets, icons, or mini-explanations unless later testing forces them

## Shot 11 — Closing callback
### Recommended reuse source
- Shot 1 preview-card silhouette
- preview/page pairing logic from Shots 7 and 8

### Safest treatment
Return briefly to the preview surface or preview-card silhouette while delivering:
`A preview can point you to a source. It is not the source.`

The visual echo should suggest recognition, not a new proof step.

### Why this is safer than a new treatment
The opening preview card is already the concept's first trust signal.
Reusing that shape at the end lets the piece close on corrected interpretation rather than new information.

### Practical design implication
- visually echo Shot 1 more than Shot 5 or Shot 6
- keep the frame clean
- avoid a recap collage of every prior element

## Transition logic across the late sequence
The safest late-sequence flow is:
1. Shot 8 reuses hierarchy language to name the mistake
2. Shot 9 flashes one brief confirming variant
3. Shot 10 clarifies the already-earned routine
4. Shot 11 returns to the preview shape with corrected trust order

This keeps the ending visibly downstream of the proof chain instead of trying to build a second center of gravity.

## What this note does not claim
This note does not claim that these shots are now visually validated.
It only records the safest current planning direction:
- reuse what is already legible
- keep density low
- avoid fresh interface invention in the ending

## Bottom line
For Shots 8, 10, and 11, visual reuse is safer than visual novelty.
The late sequence should look like a restrained echo of the proof chain, not a second design system.
