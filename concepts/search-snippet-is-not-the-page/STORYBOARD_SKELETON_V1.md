# Storyboard Skeleton V1 — The Search Snippet Is Not the Page

## Status note
This is a planning-stage storyboard skeleton.
It is not a script lock, visual lock, greenlight note, or upload recommendation.

## Working aim
Test whether the concept can communicate mainly through visible layer mismatch:
- search preview
- opened page
- update clue

## Recommended central example
Use the synthetic help-center policy pattern from `EXAMPLE_SCENES_V1.md`:
- earlier broad rule appears in the search preview
- current page includes a qualifier
- updated-date cue explains why both artifacts exist

## Sequence overview
Target shape:
1. confidence from the preview
2. friction when the page says something narrower
3. explanation through date/version evidence
4. practical checking habit

## Shot skeleton

### Shot 1 — Search result confidence
- Show a search results page with one highlighted snippet line.
- The snippet states the broader rule in plain language.
- Goal: let the viewer feel why someone would stop here.

### Shot 2 — Claim formed from preview
- Isolate the snippet more tightly.
- Add a short overlay or reaction label implying certainty.
- Goal: show the interpretive leap from preview text to conclusion.

### Shot 3 — Open the result
- Transition from results list to the actual page.
- The page heading appears first; body text is entering frame.
- Goal: create the moment where the viewer expects confirmation.

### Shot 4 — Qualifier changes scope
- Hold on the live page sentence that adds the qualifier.
- Make the qualifying phrase visually obvious inside the sentence.
- Goal: show that the page is not saying exactly what the preview implied.

### Shot 5 — Side-by-side wording compare
- Preview snippet on one side, live page sentence on the other.
- Mark the wording difference with short labels.
- Goal: make the scope shift legible at a glance.

### Shot 6 — Update clue appears
- Reveal `Updated` date or revision row associated with the page.
- Keep the date visually separate and easy to locate.
- Goal: explain how two real artifacts can disagree without claiming fakery.

### Shot 7 — Three-layer hierarchy
- Stack the three evidence layers:
  - preview
  - live page
  - update clue
- Add concise labels indicating what each layer is good for.
- Goal: teach the hierarchy visually.

### Shot 8 — Misread pattern named
- Name the mistake in compact form: treating the preview as the page.
- Keep this short and tied to the artifacts already shown.
- Goal: define the reusable pattern without drifting into generic scolding.

### Shot 9 — Quick second pattern flash
- Briefly show a truncation or embed-preview variant.
- No deep dive; just enough to show this is a class of mistake.
- Goal: widen the lesson without creating a second full example.

### Shot 10 — Viewer check habit
- Show a short checklist card:
  1. open the page
  2. compare the wording
  3. check one update clue
- Goal: leave the viewer with a concrete routine.

### Shot 11 — Closing callback
- Return to the preview line, now reframed as a pointer rather than final proof.
- End on the core line that a preview can point to a source without being the source.
- Goal: land the lesson cleanly.

## Structural notes
- The strongest hinge is Shot 4 -> Shot 6.
- If the qualifier reveal is weak, the concept weakens.
- If the update clue feels decorative, the disagreement will look arbitrary.
- Shot 9 should stay brief; otherwise the piece will lose focus.

## Small-player caution
Most fragile areas likely to be:
- the exact qualifying phrase in Shot 4
- wording comparison density in Shot 5
- the date marker in Shot 6

That means the eventual production version should prefer:
- short contrast labels
- clearly isolated phrases
- one strong date cue instead of several tiny metadata elements

## Current judgment
This skeleton suggests the concept can plausibly carry itself as a visual sequence, but that still needs to be proven in actual frames.
