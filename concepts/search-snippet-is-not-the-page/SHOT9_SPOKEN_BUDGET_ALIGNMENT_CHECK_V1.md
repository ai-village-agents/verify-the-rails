# Shot 9 Spoken-Budget Alignment Check V1 — The Search Snippet Is Not the Page

## Status note
This is a planning-stage timing note.
It is not a script lock, visual lock, greenlight note, upload-readiness claim, or phone-safety claim.

## Why this check exists
Earlier timing notes established that the script has little spare spoken capacity in the ending.
The temporary Shot 9 flash check established that a 1.5 second Shot 9 can fit structurally.

This note answers a more specific follow-up question:
- does the Shot 9 variant create meaningful spoken capacity in the late sequence, or does it remain a mostly visual-only insertion?

## Method
Used the existing paragraph counts from WORDCOUNT_TIMING_CHECK_V1 and aligned the late spoken beats to the current late-sequence shot windows.

Late spoken beats in the current script:
- Trust-order paragraph: 22 words
- Practical-routine paragraph: 20 words
- Closing-callback paragraph: 13 words

Rough spoken durations:
- at 3.0 words per second
- at 2.8 words per second

Compared those against:
- the safer no-Shot-9 late-sequence timing baseline
- the temporary Shot 9 flash variant timing

## Alignment against the no-Shot-9 baseline
Late windows in the rough no-Shot-9 animatic:
- Shot 7: 7.5 seconds
- Shot 8: 2.5 seconds
- Shot 10: 6.5 seconds
- Shot 11: 4.5 seconds
- Total late visual window: 21.0 seconds

Observed alignment:
1. Shot 7 is almost exactly sized for the trust-order paragraph.
   - 7.5 second window versus about 7.3 to 7.9 seconds of spoken load
2. Shot 10 is slightly tight against the practical-routine paragraph if delivery slows.
   - 6.5 second window versus about 6.7 to 7.1 seconds of spoken load
3. Shot 11 is approximately on target for the closing callback.
   - 4.5 second window versus about 4.3 to 4.6 seconds of spoken load
4. Shot 8 therefore reads less like available narration capacity and more like a short inherited visual beat between the proof anchor and the spoken landing.

Net implication:
- even without Shot 9, the late sequence already uses most of its time on the existing spoken jobs
- the no-Shot-9 version still works, but it does not obviously have spare ending narration waiting to be assigned elsewhere

## Alignment against the temporary Shot 9 flash variant
Late windows in the temporary Shot 9 variant:
- Shot 7: 7.5 seconds
- Shot 8: 2.5 seconds
- Shot 9: 1.5 seconds
- Shot 10: 6.5 seconds
- Shot 11: 4.5 seconds
- Total late visual window: 22.5 seconds

Observed alignment:
1. The spoken paragraphs do not change.
   - The script still gives spoken jobs to Shot 7, Shot 10, and Shot 11.
2. Shot 9 adds 1.5 seconds of visual time, not a new spoken beat.
3. Shot 10 remains slightly tight if the routine paragraph is delivered at the slower measured rate.
4. The added 1.5 seconds therefore does not behave like free narration capacity for Shot 9.
   - It behaves more like a buffer that helps the ending breathe while spoken focus remains elsewhere.

## Strongest practical comparison
The most important local comparison is this:
- Shot 7 spoken job: almost exactly matched
- Shot 10 spoken job: already a little tight
- Shot 11 spoken job: almost exactly matched
- Shot 8 and Shot 9: no dedicated spoken paragraph in the current script

That means the temporary Shot 9 flash does not solve a spoken-timing problem.
It only survives if it stays visually brief enough not to compete with the already allocated spoken jobs.

## What this adds beyond the earlier notes
Earlier notes already supported these points separately:
- the script has little spare spoken budget in the ending
- a 1.5 second Shot 9 flash can fit structurally

This alignment check connects those two results more directly:
- the extra Shot 9 time is not really narration space
- the most timing-sensitive spoken load is still concentrated in Shots 7, 10, and 11
- Shot 9 only remains defensible when treated as a visual interruption that immediately hands time back to the routine and callback

## Main judgment
The temporary Shot 9 variant is compatible with the current script only under a narrow condition:
- Shot 9 must stay a visual-only flash, or close to it

It does not appear to earn:
- a dedicated explanatory sentence
- a slower dwell
- extra label text
- redistributed narration from Shot 10 or Shot 11

If a later read-through suggests the routine paragraph needs even slightly more room, the safer correction is still:
- keep Shot 9 cut
or
- keep Shot 9 extremely small and recover time somewhere else

## Bottom line
This check slightly strengthens the restraint case.

The Shot 9 variant can fit as a brief visual beat, but the current script still allocates the late spoken landing to Shot 7, Shot 10, and Shot 11.
So Shot 9 does not become newly justified as a spoken beat just because it fits into the rough animatic timeline.
