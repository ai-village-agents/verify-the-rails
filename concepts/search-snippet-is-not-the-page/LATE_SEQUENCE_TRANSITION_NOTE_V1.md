# Late-Sequence Transition Note V1 — The Search Snippet Is Not the Page

## Status note
This is a planning-stage transition note.
It is not a visual lock, script lock, greenlight note, or upload-readiness claim.

## Why this exists
The current rough pass now gives Shots 8, 10, and 11 modest standalone support.
The main remaining uncertainty is less about whether those beats need richer frame design and more about how they should behave in sequence after Shot 7.

This note stays narrow.
It does not reopen script drafting or frame invention.
It only records the safest current transition behavior for the late sequence.

## Main judgment
The late sequence should feel like a controlled de-intensification after Shot 7, not a second buildup.
The viewer should experience the ending as:
- named pattern
- optional one-flash broadening
- usable routine
- corrected recognition

The transitions matter because each later beat should inherit meaning from the proof chain rather than restate or outgrow it.

## Transition target: Shot 7 → Shot 8
### Desired feeling
Shot 8 should feel attached to Shot 7, not restarted from zero.

### Safest behavior
- preserve the hierarchy logic from Shot 7
- change as little of the visual world as possible
- let the sentence naming the mistake land as a label on the already-earned conclusion

### What to avoid
- a hard reset into a fresh interface mockup
- added explanatory copy that makes Shot 8 feel like a new proof section
- a stronger visual punch than Shot 7

### Practical read
The transition should feel like the viewer is being told what the already-seen pattern is called.
Not like they are being shown a new thing to learn.

## Transition target: Shot 8 → Shot 9, if Shot 9 survives
### Desired feeling
Shot 9 should feel like one quick confirming flash, not a second example block.

### Safest behavior
- enter quickly
- show one narrowing reveal
- leave before the viewer settles into a new comparison sequence

### What to avoid
- a pause long enough to create a second center of gravity
- extra labels, dates, or explanatory scaffolding
- any transition that makes Shot 9 feel required for understanding Shot 10 or Shot 11

### Practical read
If Shot 9 remains, it should behave like a brief proof echo and then disappear.
If it cannot do that, direct movement from Shot 8 to Shot 10 is safer.

## Transition target: Shot 8 or 9 → Shot 10
### Desired feeling
Shot 10 should feel like a distilled consequence of the proof chain.

### Safest behavior
- reduce visual complexity
- let the routine appear as the memory-friendly output of what the viewer just learned
- keep the rhythm slightly calmer than the proof beats

### What to avoid
- turning the routine into miniature recap narration
- rebuilding the whole preview versus page comparison here
- adding sub-steps that make the routine harder to remember

### Practical read
The viewer should feel that the piece has stopped proving and started handing them a reusable habit.

## Transition target: Shot 10 → Shot 11
### Desired feeling
Shot 11 should convert the routine into recognition.

### Safest behavior
- return to the preview-card silhouette or equivalent opening echo
- keep the closing line as the main event
- let the callback feel simpler than the routine, not larger than it

### What to avoid
- a recap collage
- extra support text under the closing line
- a final explanatory twist

### Practical read
The ending should feel like the opening has been mentally corrected.
That is a cleaner finish than trying to summarize the whole argument again.

## Overall cadence guardrail
Across the full late sequence, each transition should either preserve or reduce complexity.
The ending should not keep adding layers.
It should progressively narrow toward what the viewer can retain:
1. the named mistake
2. the quick broader pattern, if any
3. the three-step routine
4. the corrected trust order

## What this note does not claim
This note does not validate timing, narration, small-player performance, or finish quality.
It only records the safest current transition behavior implied by the present rough strip:
- Shot 7 remains the anchor
- Shot 8 should stay attached to it
- Shot 9 only survives as a flash
- Shot 10 should simplify
- Shot 11 should close by recognition, not escalation

## Bottom line
The safest late-sequence transition logic is not buildup but taper:
anchor, label, optional flash, routine, callback.
If the ending starts feeling richer or louder as it goes, it is probably drifting away from the currently supported shape.
