# Script Lock Assessment V1 — The Search Snippet Is Not the Page

## Status note
This is a planning-stage script-lock assessment.
It is not a script lock, visual lock, greenlight note, or upload-readiness claim.

## Main question
Is the current script stable enough to treat as effectively locked for the next production step, or is it only a strong baseline that still needs a little more proof?

## Short answer
Not script-locked yet.
But the current draft is stable enough to keep as the working baseline.

The main reason to avoid a lock claim is not that the script feels broken.
It is that the remaining uncertainty is still concentrated in a few narrow places that should be tested before the wording is treated as settled.

## What already looks stable
### 1. Opening line
`This preview was real. The page was real. The mistake was treating the preview as the page.`

This should remain the anchor unless a later live read-through uncovers a serious pacing problem.
There is no current evidence for changing it.

### 2. Core proof wording
The script still uses the exact wording contrast that the concept depends on:
- preview: `Refunds are available for all annual plans.`
- live page: `Refunds are available for annual plans within 14 days of purchase.`

That precision is a strength, not a weakness.
The piece would get blurrier if this contrast were softened.

### 3. Update clue framing
`Updated March 14, 2026.`

The date is being used correctly: as explanation, not as debunking theater.
That framing should remain intact.

### 4. Closing routine
`open the page, compare the wording, and check one update clue`

This remains a strong habit line and still matches the evidence hierarchy well.

## Why lock status should wait
### 1. One sentence is still under mild pressure
The line about the page being `stronger present-tense evidence` is conceptually good, but it is still the one phrase most likely to sound slightly abstract in actual delivery.
That is too small a problem for a rewrite now, but it is enough reason not to declare lock yet.

### 2. The script still depends on visual execution in two places
Shot 5 and Shot 6 still need to carry real clarity:
- the wording compare has to stay readable at a glance
- the update clue has to feel causal rather than decorative

If those shots underperform, the right answer is probably not more narration.
But until that is better proven, calling the script locked would be premature.

### 3. Shot 9 is specified, but not yet pressure-tested as a full spoken moment
The second-pattern choice is now clear enough for planning.
Even so, the brief flash still has not been tested against overall pacing as a spoken-and-visual beat.
That is another small reason for restraint.

## Current best label
- strong working baseline
- good enough to keep using
- not script-locked

## What would justify a future script-lock claim
A later script-lock claim would need at least modest support from:
1. one actual spoken read-through or timing pass that confirms the trust-order line works as delivered, or clearly identifies the one sentence that should change
2. continued evidence that Shot 5 and Shot 6 can do their jobs without narration growth
3. confidence that Shot 9 stays brief enough not to pull focus from the main proof chain

## What should stay out of scope
Do not use this non-lock status as an excuse for:
- broad rewriting
- opening-line churn
- more generic source-checking advice
- stronger claims about search-engine mechanics than the example supports

## Bottom line
The script is close enough to stable that broad churn would likely do more harm than good.
But the evidence is not yet strong enough to call it locked.
The right next move is still narrow testing, not expansion.
