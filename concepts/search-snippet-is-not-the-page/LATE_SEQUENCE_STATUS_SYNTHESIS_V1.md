The late sequence now has enough separate timing and legibility checks that the main question is no longer whether there is any ending evidence at all.

The late sequence now has a credible restrained baseline, but it is supported mainly as a tapering landing rather than as a dense second half of the proof.

Shot 10 now has both legibility support and a timing-allocation decision behind it:

- its simplicity is the reason it survives
- if a later spoken pass feels tight, Shot 10 is the first justified recipient of a small extra time allocation

This makes Shot 10 the late-sequence frame with the clearest positive case at the moment.

Shot 11 now has source-based support for a narrow role:

## Current fragility map for the ending
### Stronger late-sequence assets
1. Shot 10 routine simplicity
2. Shot 11 callback structure
3. Shot 8 as a short label attached to already-earned reasoning
4. the overall taper shape of the no-Shot-9 ending

### Main late-sequence weak points that remain
1. Shot 8 does not look robust enough to carry added explanatory load
2. Shot 9 still has no strong case beyond a tiny optional flash
3. Shot 10 may still need slightly more room at slower spoken delivery

## Best current practical rules
1. Keep Shot 8 brief and visually subordinate to Shot 7.
2. Keep Shot 9 absent unless a very small flash clearly helps.
3. Keep Shot 10 simple; do not add copy.
4. If a spoken pass exposes local tightness, give extra time to Shot 10 before borrowing from heavier proof beats.
5. Keep Shot 11 clean and let the callback line do most of the work.

## What this synthesis still does not justify

- locked
- visually finished
- robust across viewing conditions
- greenlit
- upload-ready
- phone-safe enough to claim

The current support is still bounded and planning-stage.
It is simply more coherent and actionable than it was before these recent checks.
