# Storyboard Expanded V1 — The Search Snippet Is Not the Page

## Status note
This is a planning-stage expanded storyboard.
It is not a script lock, visual lock, greenlight note, or upload-readiness claim.

## Why this exists
The rough seven-frame prototype now provides enough structural evidence to justify one fuller storyboard pass.
This document expands the earlier skeleton while preserving the strongest lessons from the rough prototype:
- the concept works best when each stage has one visible job
- the qualifier frame is the main hinge
- the compare frame is useful as proof but should not become a text wall
- the update clue should explain the mismatch without making stronger indexing claims than the example supports

## Central example
Use one synthetic help-center policy page throughout.

Preview wording:
- `Refunds are available for all annual plans.`

Current page wording:
- `Refunds are available for annual plans within 14 days of purchase.`

Update clue:
- `Updated March 14, 2026`

## Target feel
- calm
- specific
- mildly surprising
- not accusatory
- not anti-search-engine
- not generic "read the article" scolding

## Story shape
The piece should feel like this:
1. the preview seems sufficient
2. the page quietly disagrees
3. one qualifier changes the meaning
4. the mismatch is shown directly
5. the date makes the disagreement make sense
6. the viewer leaves with a trust order

---

## Shot-by-shot storyboard

### Shot 1 — Preview looks sufficient
**Visual job**
Create immediate confidence in the preview layer.

**On-screen elements**
- search-result card
- title: `Refund policy — Help Center`
- URL line: `help.example.com › billing › refund-policy`
- highlighted snippet: `Refunds are available for all annual plans.`

**Motion / staging**
- enter already settled or with a very slight settle-in
- no extra busy search-page detail

**Why it exists**
The viewer should feel why someone would stop here and quote it.

---

### Shot 2 — Confidence becomes a claim
**Visual job**
Show the interpretive leap, not a lecture.

**On-screen elements**
- the same preview remains visible
- short conclusion overlay: `So refunds apply to every annual plan.`

**Motion / staging**
- overlay arrives after a short beat, as if drawn from the snippet
- avoid narrator-looking annotation arrows

**Why it exists**
This gives the later correction something concrete to push against.

---

### Shot 3 — Open the page expecting confirmation
**Visual job**
Shift from preview layer to source layer while keeping the page calm and source-like.

**On-screen elements**
- `Help Center`
- heading: `Refund policy`
- support line: `Billing and plan changes`
- full current sentence visible on page

**Motion / staging**
- page chrome and heading appear first
- body sentence enters in a way that feels like ordinary reading, not confrontation

**Important restraint**
Do not over-highlight the sentence yet.
This shot should still feel like the viewer expects confirmation.

---

### Shot 4 — The qualifier changes the scope
**Visual job**
Make the narrowing phrase feel decisive.

**On-screen elements**
- dim shared wording: `Refunds are available for annual plans`
- bright qualifier: `within 14 days of purchase.`
- optional quiet label: `Current page wording`

**Motion / staging**
- shared wording can already be present
- qualifier becomes the visual event

**Why it exists**
This is the core meaning shift.
If this shot is weak, the concept weakens.

---

### Shot 5 — Compare the two wordings directly
**Visual job**
Prove the wording mismatch explicitly without asking the viewer to parse a dense paragraph.

**On-screen elements**
Top card:
- `Preview`
- calmer shared line: `Refunds are available for`
- bright line: `all annual plans.`

Bottom card:
- `Live page`
- calmer shared line: `Refunds are available for`
- bright line: `annual plans within 14 days of purchase.`

**Motion / staging**
- simple card stack
- no extra explanatory text inside the cards

**Why it exists**
This is a proof step, not the emotional center.
It should make the mismatch undeniable after Shot 4 already made it felt.

---

### Shot 6 — One update clue explains the mismatch
**Visual job**
Make the date feel explanatory rather than decorative.

**On-screen elements**
- label: `Why the layers disagree`
- isolated date chip: `Updated March 14, 2026`
- subdued current wording retained below in smaller role

**Motion / staging**
- reveal the date after the wording mismatch is already clear
- keep the date isolated enough that the eye lands there first

**Important restraint**
Do not imply certainty about exact indexing mechanics.
The frame only needs to establish that the current page state is newer evidence.

---

### Shot 7 — Trust order / evidence hierarchy
**Visual job**
Show what each layer is good for.

**On-screen elements**
- `Preview: points to the source`
- `Live page: current wording`
- `Update clue: explains mismatch`
- bottom routine: `Open the page. Compare the wording. Check one update clue.`

**Motion / staging**
- simple stacked boxes
- bottom line arrives last

**Why it exists**
This converts the example into a reusable habit.

---

### Shot 8 — Name the misread pattern
**Visual job**
Attach a reusable label to the mistake without sounding moralizing.

**Candidate on-screen line**
- `The mistake was treating the preview as the page.`

**Motion / staging**
- keep short
- tie visually to the already shown layers rather than switching to a new interface

**Why it exists**
This turns the example into a category the viewer can notice again later.

---

### Shot 9 — Quick second-pattern flash
**Visual job**
Widen the lesson just enough to show it is not a one-off.

**Candidate variant**
- a truncated preview dropping a limiting clause

**Motion / staging**
- very brief
- do not start a second full proof chain

**Why it exists**
It broadens the lesson while preserving the first example as the main proof.

---

### Shot 10 — Viewer routine
**Visual job**
Leave a compact check routine that feels practical, not preachy.

**On-screen elements**
1. `Open the page.`
2. `Compare the wording.`
3. `Check one update clue.`

**Motion / staging**
- one step at a time or quick stack
- no long explanation under each line unless testing later proves it necessary

---

### Shot 11 — Closing callback
**Visual job**
Return to the preview with the corrected interpretation.

**Candidate closing line**
- `A preview can point you to a source. It is not the source.`

**Motion / staging**
- visually echo Shot 1
- end cleanly, without an extra twist

---

## Structural guardrails
Keep these truths intact during any later expansion:
1. **Shot 4 is the hinge.**
2. **Shot 5 must stay readable at a glance.**
3. **Shot 6 should explain with one update clue, not a metadata pile.**
4. **Shot 9 must stay short or the piece loses focus.**
5. **More shots should add clarity, not density.**

- a more causal-feeling but still restrained version of **Shot 6**
