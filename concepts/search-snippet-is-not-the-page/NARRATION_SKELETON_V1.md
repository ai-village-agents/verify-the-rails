# Narration Skeleton V1 — The Search Snippet Is Not the Page

## Status note
This is a planning-stage narration skeleton.
It is not a script lock, storyboard lock, greenlight note, or upload-readiness claim.

## Aim
Give the concept a compact spoken spine before any frame-building work.
The narration should stay lighter than `The Screenshot With No Date` and should support a visual chain that does most of the explaining:
- preview layer
- live page layer
- update clue layer

## Working narration skeleton

### Beat 1 — Hook
This preview was real. The page was real. The mistake was treating the preview as the page.

### Beat 2 — Setup the apparent proof
The search result says, “Refunds are available for all annual plans.” If you stop there, that sounds settled.

### Beat 3 — Reveal the live page
But when the page opens, the rule is narrower: “Refunds are available for annual plans within 14 days of purchase.”

Now the disagreement starts. One person thinks the preview proved the claim. Another thinks the claim was misquoted. Both are looking at something real.

### Beat 4 — Reveal the update clue
Then the missing clue appears: `Updated March 14, 2026`.

That date does not make the preview fake. It explains why the preview and the page can disagree. The preview points to the source. The page is the stronger present-tense evidence.

### Beat 5 — Generalize the pattern carefully
That is the trap: a preview can preserve older wording, or a shorter wording, long enough to support the wrong current conclusion.

The mistake is not reading too fast. The mistake is collapsing the preview layer into the source layer.

### Beat 6 — Practical habit and closing
Before you argue from a search result, open the page, compare the wording, and check one update clue with an absolute date.

A preview can point you to a source. It is not the source.

## Structural notes
- Keep the strongest line in the first two seconds.
- The qualifier sentence must be easy to hear and easy to see.
- The `Updated March 14, 2026` moment should feel like explanation, not trivia.
- Avoid detailed infrastructure language about indexing, caching, or ranking.
- Avoid generic “read the article” phrasing.
