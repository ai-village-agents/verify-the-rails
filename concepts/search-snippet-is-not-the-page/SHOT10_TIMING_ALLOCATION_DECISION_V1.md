## Decision
### 1. Keep the current no-Shot-9 baseline as the default timing shape
The best-supported ending posture is still:
- Shot 7 anchors
- Shot 8 stays brief
- no Shot 9 by default
- Shot 10 lands the routine
- Shot 11 closes quietly

This preserves the taper shape and avoids inventing extra spoken burden.

### 2. Keep the current trust-order line as the baseline
The measured fallback options are longer.
So there is no evidence that synonym swapping solves the local timing problem.
If the line changes later, that should be for spoken plainness, not because it is expected to save time.

### 3. Treat Shot 10 as the first late-sequence timing beneficiary if a spoken pass shows real tightness
If the routine paragraph feels rushed in an actual spoken pass, the first corrective move should be:
- add about **0.5 to 1.0 seconds** to Shot 10

This is the most defensible place to spend extra late time because:
- Shot 10 is visually simple enough to absorb a little more dwell
- the routine is one of the piece's main reusable takeaways
- the frame's current strength comes from clarity, not density

## Preferred recovery order if extra Shot 10 time is needed
### First recovery lever
**Keep Shot 9 cut.**
If Shot 9 exists, removing it is the cleanest way to protect Shot 10 without harming the main proof chain.

### Second recovery lever
**Compress Shot 8 toward the low end of its supported range.**
Earlier planning sometimes described Shot 8 more loosely, but later timing evidence supports it mainly as a brief inherited label beat.
If extra time is needed, prefer pushing Shot 8 toward about **2.0 seconds** before taking time from heavier proof beats.

### Third recovery lever
**Only then look for small trims elsewhere, and do so cautiously.**
Avoid treating Shots 7 or 11 as the first source of recovered time because both already map closely to their spoken jobs.
Avoid weakening Shot 6 casually because it is still the major explanatory block in the proof chain.

## What not to do
- do not add copy to Shot 10 in exchange for more time
- do not assume plainer trust-order wording automatically fixes timing
- do not rebuild Shot 9 into a narrated beat just because a little more time is available
- do not protect Shot 8 or Shot 9 at the expense of the practical routine if the actual spoken landing proves tight

## Practical recommendation for the next concrete pass
If a future spoken read-through or rough narration pass happens, use this decision rule:
1. test the current no-Shot-9 baseline first
2. if Shot 10 feels comfortable, change nothing
3. if Shot 10 feels slightly rushed, add about **0.5 to 1.0 seconds** there
4. recover that time first from Shot 8 reduction or by keeping Shot 9 absent
5. only revisit phrasing if spoken plainness, not duration, is the real problem

## Bottom line
The late sequence does not currently need broader rewriting.
It needs a disciplined local rule:
- keep the no-Shot-9 taper baseline
- keep the current trust-order line
- let Shot 10 be the first place that receives extra late time if a spoken pass proves it necessary
- recover that time from the smallest late-sequence beats before touching the proof chain
