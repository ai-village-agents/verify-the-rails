This is a planning-stage timing evidence note.
It is not a script lock, visual lock, greenlight note, or upload-readiness claim.

## Why this exists
The existing timing map used rough judgment.
A direct paragraph-by-paragraph count provides a more concrete check on whether the current script still fits the intended short-explainer shape and where the real pressure points are.

## Main implications
### 1. Overall length still fits the current concept
The measured total still supports the existing broad target of roughly **60–75 seconds** once pauses, shot transitions, and visual dwell are included.

So the main risk still does **not** look like runaway total length.
It still looks like **local allocation**.

### 2. The heaviest spoken pressure remains where expected
The densest parts are still:
- the **live-page reveal** block
- the **explanation** block
- the **trust-order** block

That supports the existing judgment that Shot 4 / 5 / 6 / 7 are where timing discipline matters most.

### 3. Shot 8 and Shot 9 do not currently have real spoken budget of their own
This is the most useful new constraint from the count.

In the current script baseline:
- Shot 7 is already carrying the trust-order sentence
- Shot 10 is already carrying the practical routine
- Shot 11 is already carrying the closing callback

That means Shot 8 and optional Shot 9 are **not** backed by clear spare narration in the present draft.

So if those shots remain in the sequence, the safest assumption is:
- they must work mostly through **visual inheritance**, **brief dwell**, or **transition rhythm**
- they should **not** quietly accumulate extra explanation lines
- if they start asking for dedicated spoken setup, they are probably stealing time from the real proof chain

### 4. Shot 9 remains easiest to cut or compress
Because there is no strong narration budget waiting for it, Shot 9 still only survives if it behaves like a true flash.
That now has direct timing support, not just stylistic support.

### 5. The ending needs to taper in both visuals and spoken load
The current measured script already puts meaningful spoken time into:
- trust order
- routine
- callback

So the late sequence should not keep expanding after Shot 7.
If the ending grows more verbally elaborate from there, it will likely feel heavier than the present script structure can support.

## Practical consequence for next validation
If a future timing pass happens, the most useful question is probably not:
- `Can the ending say more?`

It is more likely:
- `Can Shot 8 exist mainly as a visual label beat?`
- `Can Shot 9, if kept, fit as a flash without demanding new narration?`
- `Can Shot 10 and Shot 11 keep their current spoken jobs without late-sequence drag?`

## Bottom line
Measured word counts support the current short-explainer target.
They also sharpen one important constraint:
**the late sequence does not have much spare spoken capacity.**
That strengthens the case for a tapering ending in which Shot 8 inherits meaning, Shot 9 stays optional and tiny, and the real spoken landing remains Shot 10 plus Shot 11.
