# Story Outline V1 — The Search Snippet Is Not the Page

## 1. What this piece is trying to prove
A search preview can be accurate to an earlier page state and still produce a wrong present-day conclusion.

The piece should prove that disagreement often comes from checking different layers:
- preview layer (search result text)
- source layer (opened live page)
- update clue layer (last-updated note, revision timestamp, or changelog row)

## 2. Central disagreement chain
1. Person A cites a search result preview that says a rule in broad terms.
2. Person B opens the page and sees a qualifier now attached to that rule.
3. Person A assumes Person B is denying the source.
4. Person B assumes Person A is misquoting.
5. Both are partly right about different artifacts: the preview reflects older wording, while the live page reflects updated wording.
6. The real check is timeline plus layer, not who can screenshot faster.

## 3. Recommended core example
Use a synthetic help-center policy page where the earlier line was:
- “Refunds are available for all annual plans.”

Then the page is updated on `March 14, 2026` to:
- “Refunds are available for annual plans within 14 days of purchase.”

The search preview still surfaces the earlier broad sentence for a period after the update.

## 4. Six-beat outline
1. Concrete hook: show a confident claim built from a search preview sentence.
2. Layer reveal: open the page and reveal qualifying text that changes scope.
3. Timeline/version clue: expose `Updated March 14, 2026` or a revision row.
4. Evidence hierarchy: rank artifacts in-frame from preview to live page to update record.
5. Practical check habit: demonstrate a two-step routine (open page, check update clue).
6. Tight closing: repeat the main line that preview points to a source but is not the source.

## 5. Shot-role sketch
1. Search results screen with one highlighted snippet line carrying the broad claim.
2. Quick reaction frame showing how that line can feel conclusive.
3. Cursor opens the result; transition to full page.
4. Live page headline and policy block with qualifier visible.
5. Side-by-side: snippet text on left, live paragraph on right.
6. Overlay marks the exact wording delta (broad phrase vs qualified phrase).
7. Scroll or jump to “Last updated” marker.
8. Optional secondary clue: revision list row with date and short change label.
9. Collapse into three-layer stack card: Preview -> Live page -> Update clue.
10. Brief “how to check” card with two actions.
11. Return to original snippet and relabel it as pointer, not final evidence.

## 6. Viewer habit to leave them with
Before arguing from a search preview, open the page and check one update clue with an absolute date.

## 7. Risks and anti-drift rules
- Do not drift into generic “read the article” scolding; keep focus on concrete layer mismatch.
- Do not drift into generic search-engine criticism; the mechanism is caching/truncation lag, not platform blame.
- Do not make this brand-dependent; keep UI synthetic and transferable.
- Do not resolve the disagreement through narration alone; resolve it through visible artifact hierarchy.
- Do not over-index on tiny support microcopy; anchor meaning in layer transitions and dated update clues.
