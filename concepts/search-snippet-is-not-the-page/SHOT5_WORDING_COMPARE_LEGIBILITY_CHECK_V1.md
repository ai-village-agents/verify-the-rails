## Visual review takeaways
### 1. The highlighted difference survives better than the supporting text does
At reduced sizes, the most durable element is still the highlighted contrast between:
- all annual plans
and
- annual plans within 14 days of purchase

That is the right survival order.
It means the frame's main proof job remains more durable than its secondary text.

### 2. The frame is still visibly denser than the strongest single-job frames
Even in source-based review, Shot 5 asks the viewer to parse:
- two stacked cards
- two labels
- shared wording
- two highlighted endings

So although the core contrast survives, the frame still feels denser than Shot 4 and denser than the cleaner late-sequence frames.

### 3. The shared wording and labels are the first things to become expendable
At 100 percent and 75 percent, the full compare is readable for review.
At 50 percent, the highlighted endings still carry the main difference, but the smaller supporting text becomes much less comfortable.
At 35 percent, the frame still communicates that two differently highlighted phrases are being compared, but the full sentence-level read is no longer dependable.

### 4. This supports the existing storyboard logic rather than replacing it
The frame appears good enough to function as a contrast beat when it follows the qualifier reveal.
But it does not look like a frame that should be asked to do all explanatory work alone.
It still benefits from the surrounding sequence and narration telling the viewer exactly what difference to notice.

## Main judgment
This check supports a restrained conclusion:
- Shot 5 can carry the core wording contrast in the rough sequence
- the highlighted differing phrases are appropriately prioritized and survive reduction better than the supporting text
- but the frame remains meaningfully text-dependent and should not be mistaken for a highly robust small-player proof frame

## What this changes
This does not change project status.
It does slightly sharpen the existing risk statement:
- Shot 5 is not collapsing
- the compare strategy is directionally correct
- the main remaining weakness is not that the contrast disappears, but that the frame still asks for more reading than the strongest frames do

## Bottom line
The current rough Shot 5 frame keeps the main contrast visible longer than its supporting text.
That is enough to support its place in the proof chain.
But the frame is still text-heavy, still benefits from narration and prior setup, and does not justify any small-player or phone-safety claim.
