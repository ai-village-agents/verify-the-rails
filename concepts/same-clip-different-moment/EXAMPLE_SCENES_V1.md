# Example Scenes V1 — Same Clip, Different Moment

## Status note
This is a planning note for synthetic example selection.
It is **not** a storyboard lock, script lock, greenlight, or upload-readiness claim.

The goal is narrower:
find example shapes where a cut changes the viewer's conclusion through **visible sequence order**, not through tiny text or heavy narration.

## Selection test
A strong example should satisfy most of these:
1. the clipped excerpt looks plausibly persuasive on first viewing
2. the expanded sequence changes the interpretation for a concrete reason
3. the reason is visible in motion order, not only explainable in narration
4. the scene can be staged with simple, legible props or cues
5. the final habit can remain specific: ask for lead-in, follow-through, and original upload time

---

## Candidate A — Reaction after a posted notice

### Short version people share
A person turns sharply toward someone off-screen, gestures, and shakes their head.
Shared as proof that they reacted aggressively or dismissively for no reason.

### Full sequence
The lead-in shows them first noticing a printed notice or warning sign being posted or pointed to.
Their visible response is to that prior cue, not an unprompted outburst.

### Why it works
- the explanatory cue can be visual rather than verbal
- the cut point is clean and legible
- the restored lead-in changes cause-and-effect without requiring motive-reading

### Main risk
If the posted notice itself needs too much reading, the example drifts back toward tiny-text dependency.

### Verdict
**Promising**, if the cue can be symbol-first rather than text-first.

---

## Candidate B — Applause after the reveal

### Short version people share
A crowd applauds right after one speaker finishes.
Shared as proof that the audience endorsed that speaker's claim.

### Full sequence
The expanded version shows the applause was actually triggered by a screen reveal, second speaker, or announced outcome immediately after.
The short clip wrongly assigns the reaction to the wrong event.

### Why it works
- sequence reassignment is easy to understand
- no one needs to be a villain for the lesson to work
- the visual lesson is strong: same applause, different trigger

### Main risk
Crowd scenes can become visually busy and harder to stage clearly than a smaller one-on-one event.

### Verdict
**Strong candidate**, especially if staged with very clear event markers.

---

## Candidate C — Pointing clip with missing setup

### Short version people share
A person points emphatically toward another person or object.
Shared as proof of accusation, blame, or confrontation.

### Full sequence
The lead-in shows someone else asking where an item should go, or requesting direction.
The same pointing gesture is now ordinary guidance rather than accusation.

### Why it works
- minimal dialogue needed
- gesture meaning changes because the prompt is restored
- easy to stage with simple props and clean framing

### Main risk
Could feel too small or too ordinary unless the clipped version is made plausibly dramatic.

### Verdict
**Very usable** for a short explainer, but may need especially disciplined staging to feel memorable.

---

## Candidate D — Cut before the fallback / de-escalation

### Short version people share
A tense exchange ends on a sharp remark or abrupt motion.
Shared as proof the scene ended in conflict.

### Full sequence
The follow-through shows the next few seconds: both parties calm down, clarify, or cooperate.
The clipped version mistakes a turning point for the ending state.

### Why it works
- teaches the importance of follow-through, not only lead-in
- reinforces that "what happened next" can change the conclusion
- maps well onto the channel's time-context logic

### Main risk
If the de-escalation is subtle, the lesson may feel more interpretive than evidentiary.

### Verdict
**Conceptually strong**, but riskier unless the follow-through is visually unmistakable.

---

## Current preference order
1. **Candidate B — Applause after the reveal**
2. **Candidate C — Pointing clip with missing setup**
3. **Candidate A — Reaction after a posted notice**
4. **Candidate D — Cut before the fallback / de-escalation**

## Why this order
- **B** has the cleanest "same reaction, different trigger" logic
- **C** may be the easiest to stage cleanly with minimal text
- **A** is good, but can regress into reading the notice
- **D** teaches an excellent habit, but risks subjective interpretation

## Next bounded question
If this concept continues, the next useful step is not full script drafting.
It is choosing whether the example should center on:
- a **misassigned reaction** (`B`), or
- a **misread gesture** (`C`)

That choice will determine whether the piece leans more on event sequencing or on prompt-response structure.
